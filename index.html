Wheel of organisational wellbeing FECSGELD

Organisational life cycle and implementation of FECSGELD

The life cycle of an organisation typically follows several stages as it evolves, similar to the life cycle of a living organism. Irrespective of the stage in the life cycle, an organisation is expected to implement the framework based on FECSGELD effectively and efficiently. Organisations could adopt and embrace the eightfold path to wellbeing. It can be part of their charter. As organisations practice and implement the either fold path to wellbeing, their performance will improve with time as they experience cultural change. However, the intensity and the scope of application would vary depending on the life cycle stage. For example, a start-up organisation is not expected to make profit, but should have consistent cash flow to ensure its commitment to ongoing projects, and payments to suppliers and employees.

Start Up

The organization is newly formed, focusing on product development, funding, customer base, and ethical governance amid market uncertainties.

Growth

As the organization scales, it experiences rapid growth, formalizes operations, and manages complexity. It must maintain financial health, ethical governance, and responsibility to society and the environment.

Maturity

Stable organizations with steady growth and strong market positions must innovate, maintain revenue, and uphold wellbeing.

Decline

Organizations facing growth slowdown must explore new markets or products, while maintaining financial responsibility, ethical governance, and societal respect.

About Well Being

Individual wellbeing - organisational wellbeing - universal wellbeing

Individual, organisational, and universal wellbeing are deeply interconnected, with each influencing the other. When individuals experience high levels of wellbeing, they are more productive and engaged, which strengthens organisational wellbeing through better performance and collaboration. Healthy organisations contribute to universal wellbeing by adopting responsible business practices and engaging in community activities that enhance societal welfare. Conversely, when individuals or organisations suffer, it can lead to broader issues like social tensions and environmental harm, compromising universal wellbeing. Organisations that prioritise wellbeing ensure employee satisfaction and productivity, which in turn fosters a positive cycle that feeds back into individual wellbeing. Ultimately, achieving universal wellbeing requires a balanced state where the needs of individuals, organisations, and the planet are met sustainably, supporting long-term societal progress.
